\section{Overview}\label{sec:overview}

Underapproximate program reasoning---exemplified by incorrectness
logic~\cite{OHP19} and later unified with correctness in outcome
logic~\cite{ZDS23,Zil25TOPLAS}---studies judgments whose conclusions
guarantee \emph{existence} of executions rather than universal safety.
These frameworks are native to imperative programs: triples relate
commands to \emph{state-valued} outcomes.
Incorrectness logic works largely with store predicates;
outcome logic lifts assertions to \emph{weighted collections} of
outcomes (e.g.\ via semiring weights), but still composes over a
command-first operational semantics with heaps and variables in scope.
Composition therefore continues to co-evolve all parts of the visible
store unless additional structure says otherwise.

\paragraph{Detailed postconditions are no accident.}
A recurring theme in the incorrectness-logic literature is that
\emph{informative} triples are almost as granular as separation-logic
specifications: the postcondition cannot be a loose predicate on
``the output'' alone.
Because conclusions are read under an underapproximate (reverse)
consequence discipline---states mentioned in the post must be
genuinely reachable---local reasoning typically forces $Q$ to spell out
fine-grained facts about aliases, heaps, and variables that co-vary
along a command.
It is not a bug of the formalism but a structural cost of treating
everything as mutable state.
Outcome logic mitigates other pain points---such as unifying angelic
and demonic branching and reusing proof principles across
effects~\cite{ZDS23,Zil25TOPLAS}---but it does not, by itself, supply a
\emph{function-type-first} account of application.

\paragraph{Why this sits awkwardly with functional programs.}
Consider a functional program fragment
$\mathtt{let}\; y = f\;x\;\mathtt{in}\; \cdots$.
In a state-based logic, there is nothing special about the name $y$:
after reduction, the store still records $x$, and any nontrivial
incorrectness (or outcome) judgment about the composite code will
almost inevitably relate \emph{both} the new value ascribed to $y$
\emph{and} whatever is still true of $x$, often with nontrivial
correlation between the two---because, syntactically, all variables live
in the same global world.
That is entirely natural for imperative code.
It becomes less natural when the surface language is functional and the
abstraction we actually manipulate is a \emph{function type} $\tau_x
\sarr \tau_y$: programmers think they are specifying ``what $f$
returns,'' not ``how the entire lexical environment co-refines after
application.''
The mismatch is notational only if we compile closures to stores; at
the level of types, however, we would prefer to say whether $x$'s
coverage survives the call, whether it is correlated with the return,
or whether it is forgotten---without equating these with yet another
store assertion.

\paragraph{Three behaviors that pure ``output-only'' specs cannot separate.}
Suppose we only describe the return $y=(f\;x)$ and deliberately ignore
what happens to $x$.
Then, in underapproximate reasoning, we cannot discriminate three
situations that routinely arise for higher-order, coverage-driven
specifications~\cite{CoverageType}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $x$ \emph{loses} its coverage in the continuation (the argument's
  witness set is consumed or framed away during the proof of the
  application, as with magic-wand style parameter usage);
\item $x$ \emph{retains} coverage, but the witness for $x$ and the
  witness for $y$ are \emph{entangled} (they must be drawn from a
  correlated product of choices, not an unconstrained rectangle);
\item $x$ \emph{retains} coverage and is \emph{independent} of $y$
  (return coverage can be checked pointwise for every possible argument
  value, as with an overapproximate parameter modality).
\end{enumerate}
These cases are worked out in our slides (March~17); the first aligns
with incorrectness-flavored (``parameter-consuming'') application, the
last with ordinary coverage of results, and the middle is the dependent
under-type regime.
Recent concurrent and probabilistic extensions of outcome logic
(e.g.\ pcOL~\cite{ZST26POPL}) compositionally track probabilistic
\emph{independence} versus \emph{correlation} between parts of state;
that addresses a related ``rectangle vs.\ joint'' phenomenon in a
different setting (scheduler, shared sampling, separation).
Here we ask the analogous distinction for \emph{higher-order, typed}
application: whether argument coverage is consumed, entangled with the
result, or checked independently while varying only the return.
Hoare-style triples with a monolithic post on~$y$---or typing rules
that do not surface $\sarr$ versus $\wand$---still blur these three
meanings; our goal is a denotational account where they are primitive.

\paragraph{Resources as possible executions, not states.}
We therefore move from predicates on a global store to \emph{resources}:
finite collections of \emph{partial substitutions}, each representing a
consistent way the program might resolve nondeterministic or unknown
choices.
Combining resources uses a separating product (compatible glueing of
partial information) \emph{and} a choice sum (branching), so branching,
independent correlation, and framing need not be encoded as mutations
of an implicit heap.
The carrier is purpose-built for ``which execution possibilities are
we keeping track of?'' rather than ``what is the unique concrete
state?''---so the semantics does not presuppose a stateful language.

\paragraph{Choice logic and choice types.}
On this algebra we define \emph{choice logic}, a modal bunched logic
with approximation operators that play the roles of upper and lower
bounds with respect to choice.
It provides the missing logical distinctions (products,
wands, sums, and modalities) that let us express the three application
regimes compositionally.
\emph{Choice types} are the layer above: refinement constructors export
the modalities to data, while function constructors $\sarr$ and $\wand$
reify exactly the parameter/return dependency patterns listed above,
all interpreted by uniform clauses into choice logic.
