\section{Related Work}
Underapproximate program reasoning---exemplified by incorrectness
logic~\cite{OHP19} and later unified with correctness in outcome
logic~\cite{ZDS23,Zil25TOPLAS}---studies judgments whose conclusions
guarantee \emph{existence} of executions rather than universal safety.
These frameworks are native to imperative programs: triples relate
commands to \emph{state-valued} outcomes.
Incorrectness logic works largely with store predicates;
outcome logic lifts assertions to \emph{weighted collections} of
outcomes (e.g.\ via semiring weights), but still composes over a
command-first operational semantics with heaps and variables in scope.
Composition therefore continues to co-evolve all parts of the visible
store unless additional structure says otherwise.

\paragraph{Detailed postconditions are no accident.}
A recurring theme in the incorrectness-logic literature is that
\emph{informative} triples are almost as granular as separation-logic
specifications: the postcondition cannot be a loose predicate on
``the output'' alone.
Because conclusions are read under an underapproximate (reverse)
consequence discipline---states mentioned in the post must be
genuinely reachable---local reasoning typically forces $Q$ to spell out
fine-grained facts about aliases, heaps, and variables that co-vary
along a command.
Technically, one can always write a postcondition that mentions only $y$
and ignores $x$; but in the underapproximate setting, such a $Q$ is
either vacuously weak or implicitly relies on a specific $x$ that
witnesses reachability---the correlation is pushed into the semantics
rather than eliminated.
It is not a bug of the formalism but a structural cost of treating
everything as mutable state.

Outcome logic~\cite{ZDS23,Zil25TOPLAS} lifts assertions from individual
states to \emph{weighted collections} of outcomes (via semiring
weights), thereby unifying angelic and demonic branching and reusing
proof principles across effects.
Crucially, it introduces an \emph{outcome conjunction} $\varphi \oplus
\psi$ together with a trivially-true assertion $\top$, so one can write
$\langle \varphi \rangle\, C\, \langle \chi \oplus \top \rangle$ to
express that a particular outcome $\chi$ is reachable while discarding
all information about the remaining branches.
This $\oplus\,\top$ idiom is outcome logic's analogue of incorrectness
logic's ability to drop disjuncts from the post, and it is genuinely
useful for bug-finding: one need not fully characterise every program
path in order to certify that an erroneous state is reachable.

However, $\oplus\,\top$ operates on the \emph{branching} dimension of
the semantics---it selects which computational paths to keep track
of---while the problem we face is on a different, orthogonal dimension:
the \emph{argument-return dependency} of a function application.
After $y = f\;x$, the states inside $\chi$ still carry both $x$ and
$y$ in the store, and the question of how $x$'s coverage relates to
$y$'s is left entirely to the content of $\chi$.
Outcome logic therefore does not, by itself, supply a
\emph{function-type-first} account of application.

Judgment-level unifications have progressed further on the logic side:
program logics now internalize several styles in shared forms---outcome logic~\cite{ZDS23}, together with successor metatheory,
with related lifts to hyperproperties~\cite{HyperHoareLogic}.
Incorrectness separation logic supports modular local reasoning about the \emph{presence} of bugs~\cite{RBD+20}.
Complementary tooling composes incorrectness logic with sufficient incorrectness logic---pairing reachable error states with explanations of which inputs witness them---for stronger incorrectness analyses~\cite{UTurn}.

Second, refinement typing favors concise interfaces, whereas incorrectness logic often forces programmers to spell out fully explicit descriptions of reachable stores.
Incorrectness proofs often require painfully precise jointly refined poststores even when auxiliary
locations do not visibly change.
O'Hearn's canonical triple~\cite{OHP19}
\begin{align*}
    [ \top ] \; \Code{if\; \I{even}(x)\; \&\&\; \I{odd}(y)\; then\; z := 42} \; [\Code{z} = 42 \land \I{even}(\Code{x}) \land \I{odd}(\Code{y})]
\end{align*}\noindent
must mention \(x\) and \(y\) in the postcondition (preserving oddness of \(y\)) \emph{despite} their
not being assigned: omitting that guard information would unsoundly admit states such as
\([\Code{x}=0;\Code{y}=1;\Code{z}=42]\) where \(y\) is not odd.
By contrast, we would like type-level interfaces to stay short, for example
\begin{align*}
    x{:}\urt{\Int}{\top} \sarr y{:}\ort{\Int}{\vnu > 0} \sarr \urt{\Int}{\vnu = 42}
\end{align*}\noindent
(letting the result value be the third position) without spelling every relational tie between
\(x\), \(y\), and the returned integer.
Whether such abstraction is sound depends on later continuations: some uses allow treating
parameters independently (a variable of type \(x{:}\urt{\Int}{\top}\) may still be used in isolation), while others
depend on joint behaviour (so naive combinations such as \(x+z\) need not behave as if \(x\) and \(z\) were independent underapproximate draws), meaning
hidden dependency must be tracked modularly---a task that store-native presentations struggle to surface
for higher-order judgments.
